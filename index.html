65,000 leopards have been legally traded since the 2000s

A species for the hunt

Panthera pardus, otherwise known as the leopard, is one of the most trafficked big cats on the planet. They are hunted for their fur, their bones their teeth, their status as trophies, and their skin for traditional medicine. While the trade of leopards has persisted for decades, since the 2000s, over 65,000 leopards have been legally traded.

According to the IUCN, the Panthera pardus is listed as "Vulnerable" on the Red List of Threatened Species, indicating that it faces a high risk of extinction in the wild. They're only a step away from being endangered.

So, where are they coming from? Why are they being traded? Who is trading them? And how are they being traded?

Mostly from the wild

Over 37,000 of the leopard parts and bodies that legally entered the trade since the 2000s were captured directly from the species in the wild. Another 25,000 represent those that were initially confiscated or seized specimens that are now legally crossing borders for official purposes of law enforcement or education.

The scale of this legal trade alone proves just how many specimens have been intercepted by global authorities. All these numbers represent the documented and permitted buying and selling of leopards. They show just how big the market is, considering they don't even account for the illegal trade and black market.

For reasons far from noble

Leopards are highly sought after for hunting trophies and personal use. As a personal use item, you can own and use a part of the leopard as long as it's not commercial. While the commercial trade is strictly forbidden, international borders still allow the transport of legal hunting trophies under strict national quotas, creating a giant loophole for hunters to keep hunting.

Moreover, they have always been a status symbol. From the courts of ancient Egypt, the living rooms of modern day society to haute couture runways, leopards have been a symbol of luxury and exclusivity. It explains why over 38,000 leopards have been legally hunted for trophies and kept for personal use even in present day







This trend never truly stopped

Despite the decline in overall numbers, the trade of leopards remains a persistent issue, with thousands still being hunted and sold each year. Personal use dropped from over 3,000 in 2010 to just 85 in 2024 but across categories the decline has been uneven and slow.

Hunting, remains a significant and resilient driver of leopard trade. The numbers may have been improved but are far from being a solution.

Who are the top importers?

The United States is the largest importer of leopard parts in the world, driven primarily by hunting trophies and personal use. European countries follow closely, highlighting how the demand is concentrated in the wealthier nations of the world. This trend underscores the massive financial cost associated with hunting. It reinstates how leopard skin is a luxury item, sought after for its status and exclusivity.

Who are the top exporters?

Africa is the primary source of leopard exports, with countries like Namibia, South Africa, and Tanzania being the most significant exporters. It's evident that within the feeders for import of leopards, a significant portion comes from countries in Africa, where leopards are native.

However, now, The IUCN estimates only 350 mature leopards remain in West Africa. Their population has declined by over 50% in the past two decades but the exports still continue.

Namibia's leopard exports

Namibia is the highest exporter for leopard parts with over 8,000 recorded quantities. While a significant amount of these are for scientific purposes, the second majority is hunting. Considering IUCN's estimate earlier, every trophy represents a fraction of a population already on the edge of endangerment.

Most of them do not make it out alive

A small fraction of just 2% of leopards imported since 2000 arrived alive, destined primarily for zoos and private collections. The remaining 98% were brought in dead as parts of a specimen or as trophies.

They were torn apart and sold into pieces

Every part of the leopard has a market. Teeth. Claws. Hair. Bones. Skin. Turned into rugs, shoes, leather goods and medicine. Nothing is wasted.

Over 22,000 pieces were traded as derivatives and processed products made from leopard parts. Another 18,000 were traded as trophies, to be mounted and shipped to living rooms across the world.

These trades do not occur through the black market. They happen in the legal one.

Legally.
A leopard's extinction has a market.

Since the 2000s, tens of thousands of leopards have legally crossed borders as skins, skeletons, teeth, rugs, luxury goods, and hunting trophies, even while the species continues to decline in the wild.

The data reveals a disturbing contradiction: a vulnerable species can still be commercially valued, hunted, processed, and sold at a global scale under the very systems designed to protect it.

This is not an underground market hidden from the world. It is a legal one.
