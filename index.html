Leopard trade since the 2000s

Panthera pardus, otherwise known as the leopard, is a species that has been heavily traded over the last few years for various reasons, including the demand for their beautiful fur and body parts in traditional medicine. While the trade of leopards has been ongoing for centuries, the scale of the trade has decreased in recent years. And yet, since the 2000s, over 65,000 leopards have been legally traded.

According to the IUCN, the Panthera pardus is listed as "Vulnerable" on the Red List of Threatened Species, indicating that it faces a high risk of extinction in the wild.

37,000 of the leopard parts and bodies that legally entred the trade since the 2000s were captured directly from the wild. Why are they being traded?

Leopard are highly sought after for hunting trophies and personal use, wherein you can own and use a part of the leopard as long as it's not commercial. They are often seen as a status symbol, and their beautiful fur is highly prized in fashion. While commercial trade of leopard is strictly forbidden, international borders still allow the transport of legal hunting trophies under strict national quotas. Making, them hugely sought after animals and explaining why 38,000 leopards have been hunted for trophies and kept as personal use.

Since the 2000s, the trend reduced but barely ever.

Despite the decline in overall numbers, the trade of leopards remains a persistent issue, with thousands still being hunted and sold each year. The demand for their use as non-commercial objects steadily declined to 85 in 2024 samples from it's peak in 2010 at 3,000. Hunting, however, remains a significant driver of leopard trade.
